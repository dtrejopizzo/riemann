<methods>
I worked from the provided workspace files `coeffs_F9_a.npy` and `peaks_features_F1_F12_normalized_full_with_spectral_complex.csv`, plus the PDF specification document. First, I loaded the exact Ramanujan Delta coefficients `a_n` for n=1..10^6 and analytically normalized them as \(\lambda_n=a_n/n^{11/2}\), appropriate for evaluating the critical-line Dirichlet series of the weight-12 Delta L-function. I then defined the primitive quartic character mod 5 by \(\chi(1)=1\), \(\chi(2)=i\), \(\chi(3)=-i\), \(\chi(4)=-1\), \(\chi(5m)=0\), and constructed the twisted coefficients \(b_n=\lambda_n\chi(n)\), i.e. the standard automorphic twist \(L(s,\Delta\otimes\chi)\). I performed an exploratory zero search using high-precision-aware numerical partial sums on the critical strip, scanning \(t\in[0,30]\) on the critical line and at off-line real parts \(\sigma\in\{0.3,0.4,0.6,0.7\}\). This was a numerical scan only; I did not prove zero-free regions. No off-line zero candidate was found. For the peak analysis, I re-derived the correct stratification rule by matching computed values against the supplied CSV. The correct decomposition is by \(\omega(n)\), the number of distinct prime factors, not \(\Omega(n)\) with multiplicity. I verified this by reproducing the stored `abs_Sk` values exactly for F1 and F9 at known peak locations. I then implemented from scratch a Numba-accelerated Kahan-compensated summation engine for
\[
S_k(t)=\sum_{n\le N,\ \omega(n)=k} b_n n^{-1/2-it},\quad k=0,\dots,7,
\]
with \(N=10^6\). I computed a dense grid on \(t\in[10^4,2\times10^4]\) with step 0.1, found local maxima of \(|\sum_k S_k(t)|\) using `scipy.signal.find_peaks`, selected the 200 largest peaks, and refined each peak on a local 0.01 grid. From the resulting 200 complex 8-vectors \(S_k(t_i)\), I computed the class matrix
\[
M_{jk}=\frac1{200}\sum_{i=1}^{200}\Re\big(S_j(t_i)\overline{S_k(t_i)}\big).
\]
I then eigendecomposed the 8x8 real symmetric matrix and defined `pc1_complex` as the largest eigenvalue divided by the trace, and `H_complex` as the Shannon entropy of the normalized eigenvalue spectrum. I also trained two One-Class SVM models (`sklearn.svm.OneClassSVM`, `gamma='scale'`, `nu=0.05`) on log-amplitude features `log|S_k|`: first on the supplied CSV GRH-true peaks (to test compatibility with the prior detector), and second on locally recomputed F1 and F9 peaks in the same t-range as F15 to remove range confounding. A final summary figure was saved as `F15_feature_space.png`, and artifacts were saved as `F15_Sk_complex_200peaks.npz` and `F15_peaks_features.csv`.

The smallest observed moduli off the critical line in the same scan were much larger: about 0.389 at \(\sigma=0.3\), 0.218 at \(\sigma=0.4\), 0.155 at \(\sigma=0.6\), and 0.245 at \(\sigma=0.7\). - F9 (Delta): `pc1_complex` = 0.9458, `H_complex` = 0.2451 Thus F15 lies between F1 and F9 in the recomputed spectral plane. By contrast, the previously supplied full-range CSV references were:

For log-amplitude anomaly detection, the original prior-style OCSVM trained on the supplied CSV GRH-true peaks achieved AUC = 0.9087 for distinguishing the CSV GRH-true vs GRH-false peaks. But this was confounded by the mismatch in t-range, because the same prior-trained detector also misclassified locally recomputed GRH-true controls: F1 in [10^4,2\times10^4] had 30.0% anomaly fraction and F9 had 58.0%. After range-matching the training set by training OCSVM only on locally recomputed F1 and F9 peaks in [10^4,2\times10^4], the results were:

- F9: mean score 0.465, anomaly fraction 5.0%
- F15: mean score 0.154, anomaly fraction 28.5% So F15 is somewhat more atypical than F1/F9 under matched conditions, but its mean score remains positive and it is not driven into the clear violator-like regime.

<challenges>

Another important challenge was a definitional ambiguity: the user-provided description referred to \(\Omega(n)\)-stratified sums, but exact reproduction of the CSV showed the correct decomposition is by \(\omega(n)\), the number of distinct prime factors. I resolved this by exact numerical validation against F1 and F9 peak rows. A second limitation was that the supplied spectral reference coordinates for the 11 legacy classes were computed on a different peak set/range than the F15 objective requested. This created a real confound, especially for the entropy feature and the One-Class SVM. I therefore performed range-matched recomputations for F1 and F9 to make the main comparison scientifically fair. Because of time and data constraints, I did not recompute all 11 legacy classes under the new range; that limits the breadth of the matched comparison. The off-line zero search was exploratory rather than exhaustive. I cannot claim that F15 has no off-line zeros globally; I can only report that I found none in the scanned low-lying region. Also, because the construction itself is a standard automorphic twist, the initial hypothesis was mathematically suspect: such a twist is expected to remain GRH-true, so the failure to produce a violator is not surprising.
</challenges>
<discussion>
The central scientific result is negative but informative: the proposed multiplicative perturbation does not appear to generate a new RH-violator family. Instead, it produces a standard twisted modular L-function whose numerics behave like the GRH-true reference classes. In the matched spectral comparison, F15 sits essentially between zeta and Delta. That strongly suggests the previously observed violator signature is not triggered merely by multiplying Euler factors by a primitive non-quadratic character. This finding sharpens the universality question rather than weakening it. It indicates that the discriminator is not simply reacting to any nontrivial arithmetic deformation of coefficients. The known violator region seems tied to constructions that break automorphic/Euler-product structure more fundamentally, such as Davenport-Heilbronn-type additive combinations, not to honest automorphic twists. In that sense, the analysis identifies a boundary of applicability: multiplicative character twists preserve too much of the Selberg-class-like structure to mimic the spectral signature of the known GRH-violating examples. The OCSVM results illustrate an important methodological lesson already flagged in the project notes: detector performance is highly conditional on matching the peak-generation protocol. Without range matching, even genuine GRH-true controls become spuriously anomalous. Once that confound is removed, F15 looks only mildly atypical, not violator-like. Therefore the strongest evidence here comes from the direct spectral comparison, not from the naively transferred OCSVM.
</discussion>
