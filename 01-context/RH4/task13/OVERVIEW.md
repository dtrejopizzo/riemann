</challenges> <discussion>
The hypothesis is confirmed and even understated: a purely arithmetic ablation — removing integers divisible by the conductor primes 3 and 5 of F12 from the ζ Dirichlet sum — reproduces ≈ 95 % of the variance and ≈ 89 % of the magnitude of the empirical M(F12) − M(ζ) signature, with sign-perfect structural agreement across the dominant ω-bands. This is in stark contrast to the explicit-formula forward-sensitivity bridge (cos-sim ≈ −0.14, refuted in r13/r18), which sought to attribute the same difference to a single off-line zero. The implication is methodological: the ω-stratified covariance matrix M_jk acts as a *direct readout of the multiplicative structure of the Dirichlet coefficients*, not of zero locations. Two Dirichlet series whose coefficients differ primarily by a level/conductor (i.e. by which primes are "removed" or twisted at the bad primes) will produce M_jk signatures whose difference is well-predicted by the corresponding arithmetic ablation of the reference series. Conversely, this means the M_jk signature is a poor instrument for detecting RH violations *across* function classes — most of the signal differentiating, e.g., F12 from ζ is conductor/level structure, not Riemann-Hypothesis status. This is consistent with the earlier finding (r17) that cross-class held-out classifiers exhibit a "ceiling effect" tied to L-function degree/conductor rather than RH-violator status. For RH-discrimination work, the ablation model also suggests a normalization recipe: subtract (or divide out) the predictable ablation-driven component before testing residual structure for off-line-zero signatures.
