<conclusion>
Contrary to the hypothesis, the leading principal component of the peak-conditioned ω-stratified cross-term matrix M_jk has all-positive entries for BOTH the Riemann ζ-function (F1) and the GRH-violating Davenport–Heilbronn function (F4); the "constructive interference" PC1 signature is therefore not a candidate discriminator between GRH-true and GRH-false functions, although secondary characteristics (PC1 dominance, weight distribution across strata) do differ.
</conclusion> <methods>
The two pre-existing workspace files (Sk_re.npy, Sk_im.npy, t_peaks.npy) were inspected and found to span t∈[50,2000] — incompatible with the binding-document requirement that ζ-data be evaluated in t∈[10⁴,10⁵]. They were therefore not trusted, and both F1 and F4 datasets were regenerated from scratch. F1 peak finding: a Numba-parallel implementation of the Riemann–Siegel Z-function with the C0 correction term and Backlund's asymptotic θ(t) (validated against mpmath.siegelz to ~1×10⁻⁵ at t=5×10⁴) was evaluated on t∈[10⁴,10⁵] at Δt=0.05. Local maxima of |Z(t)| were detected by adjacent comparison; the top 200 by |Z| were locally refined on a 0.001-spaced grid within a ±0.05 window. F4 (Davenport–Heilbronn): the period-5 character was set with κ=0.28408 per the binding document audit (χ(1)=1, χ(2)=κ, χ(3)=−κ, χ(4)=−1, χ(5)=0). |L_DH(½+it)| was computed by truncated Dirichlet polynomial with M=10⁴ on t∈[50,2000] at Δt=0.05 and the top-200 local maxima refined on a 0.001 grid. For both classes, ω(n) was computed by linear sieve up to N=10⁶, and the ω-stratified partial sums S_k(t)=Σ_{ω(n)=k, n≤N} χ(n) n^(−1/2) e^(−i t log n) (with χ≡1 for ζ) were evaluated for k=0..7 at every refined peak using a parallel Numba kernel. The 8×8 cross-term matrix was assembled as M_jk = (1/200)·Σ_peaks Re(S_j conj(S_k)) = (1/200)·(Sre^T Sre + Sim^T Sim), and an eigh symmetric eigendecomposition produced eigenvalues/vectors. Eigenvectors were sign-normalized so the first nonzero entry is positive. Final F4 dataset saved as Sk_at_peaks_F4.npz; regenerated F1 saved as Sk_at_peaks_zeta_N1e6_regenerated.npz.

</results> <challenges>
• The workspace contained .npy files that were inconsistent with the artifact description (their t-range was [50,2000] rather than the [10⁴,10⁵] zeta range), confirming the warning in the task brief that intermediates can drift from labels; both datasets were therefore regenerated from scratch.
• Direct mpmath.siegelz evaluation was prohibitive (~0.025 s/call → ~6 hours for the required grid). A Numba-parallel Riemann–Siegel Z(t) with C0 correction was implemented and validated to ~10⁻⁵ accuracy against mpmath, reducing 1.8 M evaluations to <1 s.
• For F4, no Riemann–Siegel formula exists, so peak finding used a truncated Dirichlet polynomial; M=10⁴ was chosen as an over-truncation (≫ √(qt/2π)) that converges robustly for t≤2000.
• The k=7 stratum is intrinsically degenerate for F4 because the only ω=7 integers below 10⁶ are all divisible by 5, where χ_DH=0; this forces an exact zero column/row in M_F4.
• The leading-eigenvector test does not refute the hypothesis on technicalities — the all-positive signature is genuinely present in both functions, so the failure is substantive rather than methodological.
</challenges> <discussion>
The "constructive interference" interpretation of report r11 — that at large peaks the ω-stratified sums align constructively — is confirmed quantitatively for ζ (PC1 captures 96.7% of the second-moment variance with all-positive loading), and the PC1 vector provides a clean, low-dimensional summary of which strata contribute to peak amplitude. However, the same constructive signature also appears in the Davenport–Heilbronn L-function, which is a known counter-example to GRH. Mechanistically this is unsurprising: peak conditioning selects t where the leading exponentials approximately phase-align, and ω-stratification mixes Dirichlet coefficients across many phases within each stratum, so the dominant behavior is set by global second-moment statistics (and by the simple fact that S_k entries inflate together at large peaks) rather than by the zero-distribution properties that distinguish GRH-true from GRH-false. This is consistent with r10's pitfall: features that look discriminative when classes share the same definition collapse under class-held-out tests because they capture function-class-level magnitudes rather than a universal GRH signal. There are nonetheless quantitative differences: F4 has a substantially less-dominant PC1 (80.5 % vs 96.7 %) and a heavier sub-leading spectrum (λ₂/λ₁ ≈ 0.34 vs 0.026; λ₁/λ₀ ≈ 0.136 vs 0.021), suggesting that the fluctuation budget around peaks is more multidimensional for F4. Whether the *spectrum shape* (not the PC1 sign pattern) is a viable GRH discriminator deserves a follow-up that controls for analytic conductor and degree.
