</challenges> <discussion>
The forced-negativity surface c(δ, σ, J, T₀, m) is a sharply predictable, factorizable object. The dominant dependence is the universal δ²: at small displacement δ the off-line zero perturbs Q at second order in δ (first order vanishes by the quartet conjugation symmetry). This is exactly the behavior one expects from the Weil explicit formula expanded around the critical line — the first variation in the real part of a zero vanishes by ρ↔1-ρ symmetry, leaving a curvature term ∝δ². The prefactor c(σ, J, m, T₀) is the "detector response" that determines δ_min: the smallest displacement detectable above an external noise threshold scales as √(noise/c), so amplifying J at moderate σ ≈ 1 is the lever that pushes the detection floor downward. The m-dependence shows that the detector is local in γ-space: the operationally useful signal comes only from zeros that lie inside the basis localization window (T₀ ± O(σ√J)). This bounds, for any RH-falsification attempt, how many zeros a basis at a given T₀ must be sensitive to. Our results confirm the validation gate of §4 (α≈2.03, fits R²>0.999) and refine it to α=2.0003 ± 0.0021 in the clean differential signal, supporting the hypothesis that the negativity surface is predictable from (δ, σ, J, m, T₀) alone.
