The experiment was designed as the critical control distinguishing two hypotheses for why the L_DH Weil-form signal is so weak (α≈0.9) compared to an artificial single-zero δ-perturbation of ζ (α≈14): is it because L_DH has no Euler product (intrinsic structure), or because the L_DH off-critical pair simply has a "small enough" β/σ ratio that the Weil-form response saturates at a near-linear regime? The result decisively rejects the geometric-ratio explanation. Even when ζ is forced to carry an off-critical displacement of exactly the same β=0.3085 and probed with exactly the same σ=2.0 basis, the resulting |λ_min| grows with α≈3.76 (or 26.8× from J=8 to J=20). The real L_DH grows only 2.2× over the same range. Roughly an order-of-magnitude discrepancy persists. This places the L_DH anomaly in its own qualitative class — its mild α suggests something beyond geometry, almost certainly tied to the fact that the L_DH coefficient system (c_n = A·χ(n)+B·χ̄(n)) lacks a multiplicative Euler product, so the arithmetic-side contributions on its complete-zero spectrum partially cancel the off-critical pair's contribution in a way that an artificial ζ deformation cannot. The growth exponent α therefore is NOT a clean monotone function of β/σ alone; it depends substantively on the underlying function's analytic structure (Euler product / coefficient multiplicativity). This means α is unlikely to be a reliable universal severity indicator for GRH-type violations — at least not without first conditioning on the L-function class. The Weil form still functions as a binary detector (it correctly produces negative λ_min in both cases), but the growth exponent cannot be read off as a calibrated severity metric across function types. One important caveat: the displaced ζ zero in our experiment is 0.96 units away from the basis center, while the L_DH off-critical zero sits at the center exactly. A future replicate centering the ζ basis on γ_23 itself (T0 = 84.7355) would isolate displacement effects from off-center geometric attenuation, and is a recommended follow-up.
