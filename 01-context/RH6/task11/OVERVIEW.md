Bottleneck distances d_B(D(P₀), D(P_δ)):
- δ = 1×10⁻³ : d_B = 2.384×10⁻⁷

This matches the geometric prediction: shifting a point horizontally by δ when its nearest neighbor lies along the y-axis at vertical spacing s changes the edge length by √(s²+δ²) − s ≈ δ²/(2s), which is the quantity governing H0 merge times. With min spacing ≈ 0.0975 among the first 2,000 zeros, the predicted scale δ²/(2·0.0975) ≈ 5×10⁻⁴ at δ=0.1 is the right order of magnitude (observed 2.1×10⁻³, the merge being governed by a slightly different nearest pair).

</challenges> <discussion>
This serves as the intended sanity check for the TDA pipeline. The H0 bottleneck distance is provably 1-Lipschitz in the input point cloud (Hausdorff/bottleneck stability theorem), so any nonzero deformation must produce d_B ≥ 0 with equality only when the diagrams coincide; here we verify it is strictly positive and monotonic across three orders of magnitude in δ. Importantly, the observed δ² scaling — a consequence of the highly anisotropic geometry (zeros stack along a line) — implies that H0 bottleneck distance is a relatively *weak* detector of small real-part excursions. This quantifies a limitation that must be carried forward when using H0 bottleneck distances to compare diagrams across L-functions: small off-critical-line deviations will leave only quadratically small footprints and may be masked by other sources of variation. Higher-dimensional homology, more isotropic embeddings (e.g. (cos γ, sin γ, Re s) or normalized spacings), or Wasserstein-p distances might offer linearly sensitive alternatives.
