## Results

### Discovery 1: Localized Weil quadratic form — geometry-tuned detection of off-critical zeros with quantitative gaps to a theorem

The localized Weil quadratic form, built from the zero side minus the full arithmetic side of the explicit formula and probed with Hermite-Gauss test functions, cleanly detects off-critical-line zeros only when the basis is centered near their imaginary parts and appropriately scaled. Completing the arithmetic side and controlling truncation are essential; with these in place, the detector shows no false positives on GRH-conforming controls, exhibits δ² scaling for small deformations, and gains super-polynomial sensitivity with basis dimension.

Weil's positivity criterion links the Riemann Hypothesis to nonnegativity of a quadratic form built from test functions evaluated on the zero set and the arithmetic data of an L-function via the explicit formula. Numerically exploiting this equivalence requires constructing a bilinear form whose value vanishes when all (on- and off-critical-line) zeros are correctly accounted for; any missing off-line zero can then force a negative direction. The practical challenge is to realize this construction in a localized, finite-dimensional basis at high precision, complete the arithmetic side so that the baseline vanishes for GRH-abiding L-functions, and calibrate sensitivity so that small real-part shifts in the zero set yield robust, interpretable signals.

The core result is a geometry-tuned, control-validated detector of off-critical zeros based on the full Weil quadratic form Q = M_zeros − M_arith, evaluated on localized Hermite-Gauss test functions. Using the Davenport-Heilbronn function L_DH as the canonical GRH violation (verified to have off-line zeros near s ≈ 0.8085+85.699i, 0.6508+114.163i, 0.5744+166.479i, 0.7243+176.702i at 50 dps) establishes the negative control. A PSD-only Gram construction from on-line zeros is mathematically incapable of producing negativity (all λ_min > 0), underscoring that subtracting the arithmetic side is essential. With the full explicit-formula arithmetic side included, the detector centered at T₀≈85.7 with width σ=2 and basis size J=10 yields a strongly negative eigenvalue for L_DH (λ_min ≈ 8.995×10⁻⁴), whereas ζ, L(χ₄ mod 5), and L(Δ) produce spectra indistinguishable from zero within numerical noise (|λ_min|/trace(M_zeros) ≈ 10⁻⁸–10⁻¹⁰), cleanly separating the non-Euler-product counterexample from GRH-conforming controls.

Locality is intrinsic: away from its anomaly (T₀≈85.7), L_DH behaves like the controls, and for an artificial ζ-deformation ζ_δ that shifts Re(ρ)=1/2+δ for the first 20 zeros, significant negativity appears only when the center T₀ lies within the shifted-zeros window. Systematically scanning basis geometry quantifies this locality and sensitivity. A sweep over centers and widths shows the smallest tested deformation δ=0.05 is detected across T₀∈[12.13, 82.13] for σ=1 (and T₀∈[18.13, 80.13] for σ=2), with the detector failing outside these windows or at overly broad σ where localization is lost. At the optimal operating point (T₀=46.13, σ=1, J=10), ζ_δ produces λ_min≈0.51 while ζ, L(χ), L(Δ), and L_DH (probed away from its anomaly) remain at the 10⁻¹² floor across three independent GRH controls.

Increasing the basis dimension at fixed (T₀,σ) provides a powerful sensitivity lever: with δ=0.05, |λ_min| grows by ~13 orders of magnitude from J=4 to 20, with best-fit super-exponential scaling log|λ_min| ≈ 0.569·J·log J − 13.3; by contrast, the ζ control remains at or below the 10⁻⁸⁰ numerical floor at high precision, yielding ~90 orders of magnitude separation at J=20. Together, these results show that width, center, and basis size jointly control detectability: narrow windows centered on the target height, and richer local bases, amplify true off-line contributions without introducing spurious negativity on GRH-conforming L-functions.

The detector also calibrates deformations quantitatively. At fixed geometry (J=12, T₀=46.13, σ=1), a sweep over δ reveals |λ_min| ~ δ^α with α≈2 as δ→0, consistent with the even symmetry of Q(δ) and perturbation theory around a near-zero eigenvalue; the small-δ fit gives α=2.032±0.03 (R²=0.99987) with an asymptotic prefactor ≈610, enabling the estimate δ≈(|λ_min|/610) in the asymptotic regime. This δ² law upgrades the form from a binary test to a metrology tool and dovetails with the super-polynomial J-amplification: the minimal detectable δ falls roughly like C(J)^(−1/2), where C(J) inherits the observed exp(a√J log J) growth. Geometric tuning matters here as well: isolating the off-critical rank-2 contribution shows that the growth exponent in |λ_min| versus J increases monotonically with the normalized displacement β/σ, crossing the super-polynomial threshold (α>10) once β/σ≳2.5; this relationship is masked if the arithmetic side is incompletely modeled, emphasizing that geometry and arithmetic completeness must work together.

Completing the arithmetic side and pushing truncation far enough are critical to avoid baseline artifacts. If the arithmetic side is taken as only a short prime-power sum (e.g., Λ(n)/√n up to n≲10³) and archimedean/polar terms are omitted, the spectrum of Q is dominated by a steep, J-dependent baseline (fitted α≈21) that can swamp the off-critical signal at moderate σ, even though the off-critical-only contribution itself follows the expected monotone α(β/σ) law. Extending the prime-power cutoff by two orders of magnitude (to n≲10⁵) while including the gamma-factor and polar terms suppresses the ζ baseline by ~2733 orders across J=4–20, restoring an enormous signal-to-baseline margin (10²⁷) at all J and confirming that the earlier growth was a truncation artifact; the fitted α may still change because the residual at that point is limited by roundoff rather than truncation, but detectability is fully restored.

These engine-level findings are consistent with the classical equivalence of RH and Weil positivity, but they expose the quantitative gap: without uniform, basis-aware truncation bounds, finite-N negativity cannot be promoted to a proof of an off-line zero. The numerical facts align with rigorous structure theorems while clarifying what remains to be proved. Weil positivity (and its Guinand-Weil formulation) is equivalent to RH, and failure of RH forces the existence of test functions with negative quadratic form; constructive convolution-power arguments (Burnol) and Hilbert-space completions (Suzuki) make this explicit, while operator comparisons (Connes-Consani) suggest routes to stability under basis restriction. The missing lemma is a uniform-in-basis, explicitly quantified truncation estimate for Q that couples the localization parameters (center, width, basis dimension) to the zero-sum and prime-sum cutoffs, ensuring that the off-critical contribution dominates the tails. The present computations deliver the calibrated phenomenology — locality in T₀, δ² scaling, super-polynomial J amplification, and control-validated specificity — needed to formulate that lemma sharply and to pursue a proof in the localized setting.

### Discovery 2: ω-Class moment decomposition — finite-N growth laws and fourth-moment structure across L-functions

Partitioning Dirichlet polynomials by the number of distinct prime factors of n (ω-classes) shows that fractional second-moment allocations drift with N and therefore do not stabilize into asymptotic signatures. In contrast, ω-class second-moment growth laws and fourth-moment decompositions yield robust finite-N fingerprints that sharply separate ζ from L(Δ) and a non-Euler-product control, while highlighting strong dependence on polynomial length and time window.

Moments of L-functions on the critical line encode deep arithmetic correlations and are a central target of random-matrix heuristics and Keating-Snaith-type predictions. Numerically, these moments are probed by Dirichlet polynomials D(t)=Σa_n n^{−1/2+it} of finite length N, but such truncations mix diagonal (multiplicative) and off-diagonal (oscillatory) contributions in window-dependent ways. The ω-class decomposition — grouping terms by ω(n), the number of distinct prime factors — resolves how integers with differing factorization complexity contribute to moments and exposes how Euler-product structure, or its absence, organizes higher-order correlations. This work systematizes that decomposition to identify finite-N growth laws and fourth-moment structure across ζ, an automorphic L-function L(Δ), and a Davenport-Heilbronn-type control lacking an Euler product.

The ω-class engine decomposes D_L(t)=Σ_{n≤N}a_L(n)n^{−1/2+it} into S_k(t;N)=Σ_{ω(n)=k}a_L(n)n^{−1/2+it}, and allocates the second moment M_k(N)=∫|S_k(t;N)|²dt over a fixed window. Measured at N∈{10⁴, 5×10⁴, 10⁵} on t∈[0, 500], the fractional allocations frac_k=M_k/Σ_j M_j do **not** converge for ζ, L(Δ), or the Davenport-Heilbronn control: mass systematically migrates from low-ω to high-ω as N grows (e.g., for ζ, k=3 increases 0.339→0.458→0.486 and k=4 increases 0.024→0.063→0.084, while k=1 falls 0.140→0.061→0.045). This drift is explained by the Erdős-Kac law (typical ω(n)≈log log N), which biases the integer pool toward higher ω as N increases; ζ inherits the drift most strongly because |a_n|²/n diverges logarithmically for a_n≡1, while L(Δ) and the Davenport-Heilbronn control exhibit slower redistribution despite near-convergent totals. Importantly, diagonal-only approximations to ∫|S_k|²dt are inaccurate at T=500 (163–3% discrepancies at N=2000), so the study integrates |S_k|² by direct time sampling to capture off-diagonal cross-terms faithfully.

To extract robust finite-N structure despite the fractional drift, the growth of class moments M_k(N) with N was fit to powers of log N up to N=10⁶. Across ω-classes k=1–5, ζ exhibits dramatically larger exponents than L(Δ) and the Davenport-Heilbronn control — for example, a_ζ≈5.910, 9.179, 11.260, 14.831, 19.285 versus a_L(Δ)≈0.289, 0.798, 2.023, 4.821, 9.949 and a_DH≈0.171, 0.480, 1.566, 3.066, 8.611, with separation ratios ranging from ≈20–35× at k=1 down to ≈1.9–2.2× at k=5 (all fits over t∈[0, 500], R²≥0.97 in almost all cases). Adding N=10⁶ increases ζ's exponents relative to three-point fits, indicating ζ's growth is faster than a pure (log N)^a law, but the ζ-vs-controls separation strengthens rather than weakens. A two-parameter refinement log M_k = log C + a·log log N + b·log log log N drives R² to 0.9991–1.000 and reveals a coherent sign pattern has a>0 and b<0 across ζ classes, while L(Δ) and the Davenport-Heilbronn control have a≈0 and b>0 — but with only four N values the fitted (a, b) drift substantially when refit on a three-point subset, so these exponents should be treated as descriptive rather than invariant at present.

The fourth moment ∫|D_L|⁴dt was decomposed into pure class terms Σ_k∫|S_k|⁴dt and diagonal cross-class terms 4Σ_{j<k}∫|S_j|²|S_k|²dt (the factor 4 accounts for the four ordered (j,j,k,k)-tuples), with the off-diagonal residual defined as total minus these two contributions. At N=10⁴ on t∈[0, 500], the diagonal share is only 17.742% for ζ versus 74.196% for L(Δ) and 103.061% for the Davenport-Heilbronn control (the latter's off-diagonal residual is 3.061%), demonstrating a marked structural split between Euler-product and non-Euler-product series. The dominant diagonal pairs differ accordingly: ζ concentrates on (2,3), while L(Δ) is led by (1,2) and (0,1); the Davenport-Heilbronn control shows heavy coupling to class 0, reflecting the lack of multiplicativity. The negative residual in the non-Euler-product case is expected from destructive interference and is not a numerical artifact; more broadly, the large off-diagonal share for ζ in this low-t window is consistent with strong oscillatory cross-terms that are under control only asymptotically when Euler-product divisor structures dominate.

Shifting the window to t∈[500, 1000] removes the t≈0 spike that dominated ζ's fourth moment on [0, 500] and reveals strong window dependence: ζ's off-diagonal residual collapses from +82.26% to 8.63% (≈90.9 percentage points), while L(Δ) and the Davenport-Heilbronn control move in the opposite direction, increasing their off-diagonal shares to +37.45% and +22.56%, respectively. Extending to N=5×10⁴ on the same height window shows the three functions evolving in different directions: ζ's residual becomes more negative (−32.17%), L(Δ) remains essentially flat (~+39.84%), and the Davenport-Heilbronn control shrinks toward zero (+5.15%) — underscoring that all are in finite-N, window-dependent regimes rather than converging uniformly to a common diagonal-dominant picture.

Taken together, these results deliver two complementary, robust finite-N signatures: (i) sharply separated ω-class second-moment growth exponents that distinguish ζ from L(Δ) and a non-Euler-product control, and (ii) fourth-moment diagonal/off-diagonal structure that tracks Euler-product multiplicativity versus its absence — while simultaneously emphasizing that larger N and higher-t windows are required to connect cleanly to Keating-Snaith-type asymptotics for Euler-product L-functions.

### Discovery 3: H0 persistence is quadratically insensitive to real-part displacements of zeros

H0 persistence on point clouds of zeta and L-function zeros increases monotonically but only quadratically with small real-part shifts δ, making it a weak detector of off-critical-line zeros. Both bottleneck and 1-Wasserstein distances on H0 persistence diagrams scale as δ² and fail to flag the Davenport-Heilbronn function as an outlier at matched sample size, motivating a shift toward more sensitive arithmetic detectors.

Understanding how to detect deviations from RH in finite data is a central problem at the interface of analytic number theory and computation. One geometric approach treats zeros as a point cloud and compares their topological signatures under perturbations, using persistent homology and standard distances between persistence diagrams. While such methods are robust and model-agnostic, their utility depends on how deformations of the zero set propagate to birth/death scales controlling H0 connectivity. The present study quantifies this propagation for small horizontal (real-part) displacements and tests whether it is strong enough to separate a known RH-violating L-function from standard GRH-conforming families.

The study establishes that H0 persistence-diagram distances respond only at second order to small horizontal shifts of a subset of zeros. Using the first 2,000 Riemann ζ zeros as a baseline cloud P₀={(0.5,γ)}, deforming the first 20 zeros to P_δ={(0.5+δ,γ)} with δ∈{10⁻³,10⁻²,10⁻¹} produces strictly increasing bottleneck distances d_B(P₀, P_δ) = 2.384×10⁻⁷, 2.289×10⁻⁵, and 2.082×10⁻³, respectively, with a log-log slope ≈1.97 indicating quadratic scaling d_B~δ². The same experiment with the 1-Wasserstein distance W₁ between H0 diagrams yields W₁(P₀, P_δ) = 2.384×10⁻⁷, 2.289×10⁻⁵, and 2.279×10⁻³, with fitted slope ≈1.99, again confirming quadratic scaling. In both metrics the essential (infinite-death) H0 class was excluded, and float64 precision was verified to be adequate; recomputing via an explicit float64 distance matrix reproduced results, ruling out precision artifacts.

The mechanism is geometric. Zeros within each cloud lie nearly on a vertical line, so the edges determining H0 merge times are almost vertical. Shifting one endpoint horizontally by δ changes a typical interpoint distance from s to √(s²+δ²), and hence alters the edge length, and therefore the relevant H0 birth/death scale, by ~δ²/(2s). Because within-shifted and within-unshifted pairs are unchanged and only cross pairs shift by O(δ²), every birth/death coordinate in the H0 diagram moves by at most O(δ²), forcing any diagram distance based on these coordinates (bottleneck, Wasserstein) to scale quadratically in δ. The measured slopes near 2 match this prediction across three decades in δ, quantifying H0's intrinsic insensitivity to small real-part displacements in this embedding.

With this limitation characterized, the method was stress-tested on a function known to violate RH: the Davenport-Heilbronn L-function L_DH(s), which satisfies a symmetric functional equation but lacks an Euler product. Its definition was verified by reconstructing L_DH(s) = ((1−iκ)/2)L(s,χ) + ((1+iκ)/2)L(s,χ̄) with κ≈0.284079 and confirming four anchor off-critical zeros via Newton refinement to |L_DH|<10⁻⁴⁹ at Re(s)∈[0.574, 0.809], validating it as a canonical negative control. This ensured that any detector sensitive to off-line zeros should, in principle, separate L_DH from GRH-conforming L-functions at comparable heights.

In a like-for-like TDA comparison restricted to the first 122 zeros of each function (matching the available L_DH sample), pairwise H0 bottleneck distances showed that L_DH was **not** an outlier: the mean distance of L_DH to the other three functions was 1.402, comparable to L(χ)=1.298 and L(Δ)=1.192, and much smaller than ζ=2.471, which appeared spuriously isolated due to a larger height span at fixed index. Injecting the known off-critical L_DH zero at (0.808517, 85.699348) changed its own diagram by only 0.375, on the same scale as natural cross-function variation. Exact and randomized permutation tests yielded p≈0.50 and p=0.478, respectively, indicating no statistically significant separation of L_DH by H0 bottleneck distance under these conditions. These empirical negatives are consistent with the δ² attenuation quantified above and with confounding from height-range differences at fixed zero counts.

Together, these results demonstrate that H0 persistence on (Re, Im) zero clouds is a monotone but quadratically weak probe of small off-critical displacements: its signal scales as δ² and is easily masked by ordinary between-family variability in zero spacings and sampling windows. The analysis suggests concrete remedies if TDA is to contribute: anisotropic embeddings that amplify the real part, height-matched comparisons, or shifting to features whose birth/death depend linearly on horizontal offsets (e.g., H1 cycles in suitable filtrations). In parallel, detectors grounded in the explicit formula, such as localized Weil quadratic forms, remain the more sensitive instruments for distinguishing RH-adherent from RH-violating behavior in finite computations, as already indicated in complementary analyses.
