<conclusion>
The fractional ω-class second-moment contributions do NOT converge to stable values over N_terms∈{10⁴,5×10⁴,10⁵} for any of the three L-functions (ζ, L(Δ), L_DH); mass systematically migrates from low-ω classes (k=0,1,2) to higher-ω classes (k=3,4,≥5) as N grows, consistent with the Erdős–Kac drift of ω(n)~log log n.
</conclusion> <methods>
1. Reconstruction of the ω-class engine. The referenced "report r8" engine and zero-list .npy files were absent from the workspace; only `ldh_def.py` was present. I therefore reconstructed the canonical ω-class decomposition of a Dirichlet polynomial S(t)=Σ_{n=1..N} a_n n^{-1/2-it}, partitioning the index set by ω(n) (number of distinct prime divisors), giving S(t)=Σ_k S_k(t) with S_k(t)=Σ_{ω(n)=k} a_n n^{-1/2-it}.
2. Coefficients (analytic normalization, so the functional equation is symmetric about Re(s)=1/2): - ζ: a_n = 1. - L(Δ): a_n = τ(n)/n^{11/2}, with τ(n) computed via PARI/GP (`cypari2.Pari.ramanujantau`) for n=1..10⁵ in ~1 s. - L_DH: a_n = A χ(n) + B χ̄(n), with χ the primitive Dirichlet character mod 5 of order 4 and A,B,ξ_DH from `ldh_def.py`. I proved a_n is real and reduces in closed form to a_n∈{1,ξ,−ξ,−1,0} depending on n mod 5; verified against the mpmath construction to 50 dps.
3. ω(n) was computed by a smallest-prime-factor sieve up to N_max=10⁵.
4. Moment integration. Because off-diagonal cross terms in ∫_0^T|S_k|² dt are NOT negligible at T=500 (a direct sanity check at N=2000, T=500 showed diagonal-only fractions differ from the exact quadratic-form fractions by 16–33 %), I evaluated the integrals by direct sampling: S_k(t) was computed by vectorised numpy on a uniform t-grid with Δt=0.05 over [0,500] (10 001 samples; ≥4× Nyquist for the highest log-frequency log(10⁵)/2π≈1.83), then |S_k(t)|² integrated by the trapezoidal rule. Sampling was chunked in batches of 100–200 t-values to keep memory <2 GB per chunk. The method was validated at N=2000 by comparison with the exact quadratic-form integral Σ_{m,n} c_m c_n sin(log(m/n)T)/log(m/n); agreement to printed 6-digit precision.
5. M_k was computed for k=0,1,2,3,4 and k≥5 (lumped) for each L-function and each N∈{10⁴,5×10⁴,10⁵} (9 integrations; ~7 min total wall time on a single CPU). Fractions frac_k = M_k/Σ_j M_j were tabulated and the relative drift between successive N values was used as the convergence diagnostic.

</challenges> <discussion>
The hypothesis that the ω-class fractional second moments stabilise with N_terms is rejected for the tested range N≤10⁵. The driver is the well-known Erdős–Kac phenomenon: the typical ω(n) for n≤N grows as log log N (≈2.44 at N=10⁵), so the ω-distribution of integers in [1,N] is itself slowly drifting; any moment-weighting that does not perfectly cancel this drift will inherit it. Empirically, ζ inherits the drift most strongly because its coefficient sum Σ|a_n|²/n diverges, giving most weight to the largest n in the polynomial. The cusp form L(Δ) and L_DH have bounded |a_n|² on average (Rankin–Selberg / character-sum cancellation), so their total moments are converging — total Σ M_k drifts only ~3.7 % (L_DH) and ~3.4 % (L(Δ)) from N=5×10⁴ to 10⁵ — yet the *redistribution* among classes still proceeds because higher-ω integers are simply being added to the polynomial as N grows. Implication for the "report r8" claim: a finding of a fixed ω-class signature at any single N (e.g., the original observed distribution) is essentially measuring the ω-distribution of {1..N} weighted by |a_n|² rather than a robust structural property of the L-function. To make a structural claim one would need either (a) a normalisation that removes the Erdős–Kac drift (e.g., comparing each L-function against ζ at the same N, holding the ω-weights fixed), or (b) pushing N high enough that frac_k stabilises against further drift — which, given that k=4 has not yet stabilised at N=10⁵, would require N at least 10⁸–10¹⁰ for a convincing limit. Across L-functions, however, the *ordering* of classes is consistent at fixed N: L(Δ) is concentrated at k=1 (≈43 %), L_DH at k=2 (≈36 %), and ζ at k=2/3 (≈38–49 %), with L_DH and ζ both showing rapid k=4 growth. This qualitative pattern, not the fractions themselves, may be the more meaningful comparison.
