<conclusion>
Yes: for normal/self-adjoint T there are general, practically checkable operator-algebraic criteria—via bicommutants and the (Borel/L∞) functional calculus—that can certify a nonzero operator L lies outside W*(T).
</conclusion>
<explanation>
The key structural tool is that the von Neumann algebra generated by a bounded operator T is the bicommutant W*(T)=VN(T)={T,T*}′′, and membership can be tested through commutants: L∈W*(T) iff L commutes with every operator that commutes with T. Consequently, to prove non-membership it suffices to produce a single “witness” S∈{T}′ with [S,L]≠0. This is an explicit, formal certificate because it directly contradicts L∈{T}′′. (blackadar2017operatoralgebras pages 55-59)

When T is normal (in particular self-adjoint), W*(T) is abelian and is described by the spectral theorem/functional calculus: the spectral projections E_B(T) (Borel sets B) lie in {T}′′ and generate the same von Neumann algebra; equivalently, W*(T) consists of bounded Borel (or essentially bounded measurable) functions of T. In a spectral representation, T is unitarily equivalent to multiplication by the coordinate function, and then W*(T) corresponds to multiplication by bounded measurable functions. This yields a second family of certificates: if there exists a Borel set B with [E_B(T),L]≠0 (i.e., L does not preserve the corresponding spectral subspace), then L∉W*(T). (blackadar2017operatoralgebras pages 30-34, blackadar2017operatoralgebras pages 34-38)

The commutation-relations hypothesis in the objective is addressed cleanly in the normal/self-adjoint case: since every element of W*(T) commutes with T, any nontrivial “ladder” relation [T,L]=cL with c≠0 forces L∉W*(T), unless L=0 (because L∈W*(T) would imply [T,L]=0, hence cL=0). Thus a Tate-twist-style relation provides an immediate Independence Gate check under the assumption T is normal/self-adjoint (or more generally whenever W*(T) is abelian). (blackadar2017operatoralgebras pages 55-59, budde2015operatoralgebrasand pages 71-74)

Spectral-type and Hilbert-space-structure enter through the multiplication/direct-sum model: for separable normal operators, the spectral theorem expresses T as (a direct sum/direct integral of) multiplication operators on L2-spaces, making W*(T) “diagonal” (decomposable) in that model. Therefore, any L that mixes distinct spectral fibers, shifts support across disjoint spectral regions, or acts off-diagonally in the spectral decomposition cannot be a multiplication operator and hence cannot belong to W*(T). This “decomposability obstruction” is the operator-algebraic formulation of “L depends on independent geometric data not measurable with respect to T’s spectral variable.” (blackadar2017operatoralgebras pages 34-38, blackadar2017operatoralgebras pages 15-19)

Practical synthesis (Independence Gate):
1) Commutator gate: compute [T,L]. If nonzero, conclude L∉W*(T) (in the normal/self-adjoint setting). In particular, verify whether [T,L]=cL with c≠0; if yes and L≠0 then L∉W*(T). (blackadar2017operatoralgebras pages 55-59, budde2015operatoralgebrasand pages 71-74)
2) Spectral-projection gate: find a Borel set B such that the spectral projection E_B(T) does not reduce L; equivalently show [E_B(T),L]≠0. Then L∉W*(T). (blackadar2017operatoralgebras pages 30-34, blackadar2017operatoralgebras pages 34-38)
3) Bicommutant-witness gate: construct S∈{T}′ (often coming from multiplicity spaces/symmetries in the spectral model) with [S,L]≠0; then L∉W*(T). (blackadar2017operatoralgebras pages 55-59)

No statistical tests were performed because the question is purely mathematical (the “certification” is a proof obligation, not an empirical inference). The scientific/mathematical implication for the Riemann-zero-spectrum hypothesis is: if T is modeled as a self-adjoint operator with “Riemann-zero-like” spectrum (hence normal), then W*(T) is abelian and extremely rigid (L∞-functional calculus). Any geometrically defined Lefschetz-type operator L that either (i) fails to commute with T or (ii) fails to be decomposable with respect to the spectral decomposition of T can be formally certified as independent (not in W*(T)). (budde2015operatoralgebrasand pages 71-74, blackadar2017operatoralgebras pages 34-38)
</explanation>
<discussion>
The strongest tools found apply when T is bounded and normal/self-adjoint; in this regime, W*(T) is abelian and identified with an L∞ functional calculus, so “independence” reduces to showing L is not a measurable function of the spectral variable. The commutator criterion [T,L]=cL with c≠0 is particularly decisive, but it presumes one can rigorously define and compute the commutator on a suitable domain. For unbounded operators or non-normal T, W*(T) may be non-abelian and the above simplifications weaken; nonetheless, the bicommutant witness method (find S commuting with T but not with L) remains valid in general because it is purely von Neumann algebraic.
