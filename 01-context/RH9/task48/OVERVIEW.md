It also records which prerequisites fail for `Spec \mathbb{Z}`, isolating the main obstructions.*

Foundational sources uniformly set up the Fourier–Mukai (FM) transform for an abelian variety \(A\) (or abelian scheme \(A\to S\)) by using a universal line bundle (Poincaré bundle) \(\mathcal P\) on \(A\times \hat A\) (or \(\hat A\times A\)) as the kernel of an integral transform; e.g. the derived-category FM functor is of the form \(\Phi_{\mathcal P}(\bullet)=Rq_*\big(p^*(\bullet)\otimes \mathcal P\big)\), and Beauville explicitly uses this kernel formalism to connect derived correspondences to endomorphisms of Chow groups (beauville2008theactionof pages 1-4, beauville2008theactionof media 3251a986). In the standard account, \(A\) is a projective connected commutative algebraic group (hence smooth), and \(\hat A\) is introduced as the representing object for \(\mathrm{Pic}^0(A)\), the translation-invariant part of the Picard functor; i.e. representability of \(\mathrm{Pic}^0\) is a key geometric prerequisite for forming a “dual” and for even stating universality of \(\mathcal P\) (quiccioneUnknownyearderivedcategoriesand pages 62-65, huybrechts2006fouriermukaitransformsin pages 203-207).

The central theorem-level input for the Poincaré bundle is its universal property together with a normalization (rigidification) along the identity section. Huybrechts states that the Poincaré bundle \(\mathcal P\), if it exists, is characterized by fibrewise conditions \(\mathcal P|_{\{\alpha\}\times A}\cong L\) (for \(\alpha\in \hat A\) corresponding to \(L\in \mathrm{Pic}(A)\)) and trivialization on \(A\times\{e\}\); uniqueness is immediate from the see–saw principle (huybrechts2006fouriermukaitransformsin pages 200-203). He then states the stronger representability result: \((\hat A,\mathcal P)\) represents the functor \(\mathrm{Pic}^0_A\), and any family \(\mathcal M\) of \(\mathrm{Pic}^0\)-line bundles on \(S\times A\) is (uniquely up to twisting from \(S\)) pulled back from \(\mathcal P\) by a unique morphism \(f_\mathcal M:S\to \hat A\); moreover, if \(\mathcal M\) is normalized so that \(\mathcal M|_{S\times\{e\}}\) is trivial, then the pullback is an actual isomorphism (not just up to base twist) (huybrechts2006fouriermukaitransformsin pages 203-207). Quiccione’s exposition records the same package as an explicit existence/uniqueness theorem (Thm 4.3.4) and highlights translation-invariance criteria (a theorem-of-the-square style condition) that underpin the construction (quiccioneUnknownyearderivedcategoriesand pages 62-65). Bunke similarly foregrounds the refined see–saw principle and theorems of the cube/square as the tools controlling families of line bundles under addition/translation—exactly the identities needed to normalize and verify compatibility relations for universal bundles on products of group varieties (bunke2005abelianvarietiesand pages 1-6).

For Chow groups and the \(\mathfrak{sl}_2\) package, the literature does not treat the lowering operator as an abstractly postulated map but as arising from (i) an action of \(SL_2\) (or its Lie algebra) realized by algebraic correspondences/kernels and (ii) the Fourier transform interchanging convolution (Pontryagin) and intersection products. Beauville constructs an algebraic \(SL_2\)-action on (rational) Chow groups of an abelian variety \(A\) starting from a polarization \(\theta\) and the associated Poincaré bundle class (pulled back from \(A\times \hat A\)); he explicitly expresses the Chow class of the Poincaré bundle in terms of \(p^*\theta,q^*\theta\) and the addition map \(m^*\theta\), and uses FM kernels \(K\in D(A\times A)\) to produce functors and then operations on Chow via Chern character and correspondence action (beauville2008theactionof pages 1-4, beauville2008theactionof media 3251a986). Polishchuk formulates analogous constructions over a base \(S\) for abelian schemes, again taking as given the dual abelian scheme \(\hat A\) and the normalized Poincaré bundle \(\mathcal P\), and uses theta/Mumford group machinery plus “intertwining operators” to build the representation-theoretic operators corresponding to \(SL_2\)-type symmetries (polishchuk1997analogueofweil pages 1-3). Moonen–Polishchuk emphasize that at the Chow level the Fourier transform is fundamentally a transform for commutative group/monoid schemes with proper multiplication (to define Pontryagin products), again indicating that the group-scheme geometry is the enabling prerequisite for the differential-operator/raising-lowering formalism (moonen2010dividedpowersin pages 1-3).

Each implication is explicitly present in the cited sources (huybrechts2006fouriermukaitransformsin pages 203-207, huybrechts2006fouriermukaitransformsin pages 200-203, beauville2008theactionof pages 1-4, polishchuk1997analogueofweil pages 1-3).

Finally, regarding obstructions in the absence of such geometry, Donagi–Pantev give a clean model example: for a genus-one fibration \(Y\to B\) without a section, even though one can form a relative Jacobian \(X:=\mathrm{Pic}^0(Y/B)\), the moduli problem is not fine and “we do not have a Poincaré sheaf on \(Y\times_B X\)”; instead the obstruction is encoded by a nontrivial \(\mathcal O^\times\)-gerbe/Brauer class, motivating twisted (gerby) Fourier–Mukai transforms (donagi2008torusfibrationsgerbes pages 1-4). This illustrates that even slight relaxations of the abelian-scheme hypotheses can destroy the existence of an honest Poincaré kernel.

Putting these dependencies against \(\mathrm{Spec}\,\mathbb Z\): it is not an abelian variety/scheme, lacks a proper commutative group law, has no dual abelian variety representing \(\mathrm{Pic}^0\), and hence there is no candidate for a normalized Poincaré bundle on \(X\times \hat X\) in the relevant sense. Therefore the particular Fourier–Mukai-based construction of the lowering operator cannot even be initiated for \(\mathrm{Spec}\,\mathbb Z\) (huybrechts2006fouriermukaitransformsin pages 203-207, quiccioneUnknownyearderivedcategoriesand pages 62-65, polishchuk1997analogueofweil pages 1-3).

<discussion>
The survey evidence consistently treats the Poincaré bundle as the enabling geometric object: it is the universal family over \(\hat A\) representing \(\mathrm{Pic}^0\) and the kernel that defines the FM transform (huybrechts2006fouriermukaitransformsin pages 203-207, huybrechts2006fouriermukaitransformsin pages 200-203). The \(\mathfrak{sl}_2\) lowering operator (in the Beauville–Künnemann–Polishchuk tradition) is then built from explicit correspondences induced by this transform together with polarization data and the group-law-induced Pontryagin formalism (beauville2008theactionof pages 1-4, polishchuk1997analogueofweil pages 1-3, moonen2010dividedpowersin pages 1-3). In that sense, the hypothesis is confirmed at the level of logical dependence: removing the Poincaré bundle (or the prerequisites needed to guarantee its existence) removes the construction.

Limitations: (i) the extracted texts emphasize the abelian-variety/scheme case, so “minimality” is relative to that literature; there are broader contexts (e.g., twisted FM transforms on gerbes, or FM-type kernels on other dualities) where analogues exist, but those constructions typically require replacement data (gerbes/Azumaya algebras, fine moduli conditions, or other duality objects) (donagi2008torusfibrationsgerbes pages 1-4, donagi2008torusfibrationsgerbes pages 16-19). (ii) the present evidence set does not include the original Künnemann paper where the Lefschetz \(\mathfrak{sl}_2\)-action is constructed motivically; instead it uses Beauville and Polishchuk expositions that reference that tradition (beauville2008theactionof pages 1-4, polishchuk1997analogueofweil pages 1-3). (iii) the discussion of \(\mathrm{Spec}\,\mathbb Z\) obstructions is necessarily structural rather than theorem-citing, because the retrieved sources are about abelian varieties/schemes; nonetheless, the obstruction list is a direct negation of the stated hypotheses used in those theorems.
