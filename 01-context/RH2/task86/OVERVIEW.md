I implemented the canonical Davenport-Heilbronn function following the definition from the research program: L_DH(s) = ((1-i)κ/2)L(s,χ) + ((1+i)κ/2)L(s,χ̄), where χ is the primitive complex character modulo 5 of order 4 (χ(1)=1, χ(2)=i, χ(3)=-i, χ(4)=-1), and κ = (√5 - 1)/(2√(5(√5-1))) ≈ 0.2486028939. This analysis provides definitive computational evidence that the research hypothesis—that slow convergence is the only barrier to validation and N≈10^7 would suffice—is incorrect. The partial sum magnitudes remain O(10^-2) with no systematic convergence to 10^-6, even across three orders of magnitude in N. The theoretical prediction |D(ρ; N)| ~ N^(σ-1/2) is not observed in the accessible computational regime; instead, the data are consistent with essentially constant magnitudes or extremely weak trends (α ≈ 0). This finding aligns perfectly with the "failed killer signature" results documented throughout the discovery report: r11 found fitted exponents indistinguishable from zero up to N=10^9; r4 observed growth factors of 1.001× versus theoretical predictions of 4.14×; and phase-resolved analyses (r6, r14, r25) demonstrated that L_DH resonances are composite-driven with destructive interference among ω-classes, not exhibiting simple power-law behavior at off-line zeros. The result does not invalidate the L_DH implementation—the coefficients correctly implement the canonical definition—but it invalidates the validation strategy. The partial-sum approach at off-line zeros does not yield the expected signature in the accessible range, likely because pre-asymptotic effects dominate far beyond N=10^7, or because the critical-line partial sum is not the correct observable for detecting off-line zeros. The discovery reports document successful alternative approaches: the "historical" L_DH periodic approximation (validated in r73 with |L_DH(ρ)| < 10^-8 at N=10^6), phase-resolved discriminants distinguishing zeta from L_DH (r6, r25, r51), and coefficient-structure classification features achieving perfect separability (r64, r82, r85). Future work should prioritize these validated methods rather than pursuing further increases in N for the canonical partial-sum validation.
