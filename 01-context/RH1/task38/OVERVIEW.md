The hypothesis is **REJECTED**. The R_comp metric's scaling behavior with truncation length N is NOT primarily determined by coefficient periodicity. The scaling behavior may differ at non-peak locations or averaged over t ranges. ## Statistical Evidence Using log-log linear regression, each function's R_comp(N) follows R_comp = c × N^α with high confidence (all p < 10⁻⁸ for the slope parameter). The predicted separate behaviors (α ≈ 0 for periodic, α ≈ -0.5 for random) fall well outside the 95% confidence intervals for all observed exponents. ## Interpretation The R_comp metric (mean resultant length of composite squarefree terms) decays as N increases for ALL functions, regardless of coefficient periodicity. This decay likely reflects the increasing phase decoherence as more terms are included, even at peak locations. The hypothesis that periodic coefficient structure would maintain coherence (yielding α ≈ 0) is not supported. The modest difference between groups (periodic functions decay ~5% faster) suggests that while periodicity influences R_comp scaling, it is not the primary structural driver. Other factors dominating the behavior include:
- Specific arithmetic properties of the coefficients
- Character modulus and conductor properties
- The choice of height t (peaks vs. typical values)
- The definition and normalization of R_comp itself ## Recommendation The R_comp scaling exponent α should not be used as a primary diagnostic to distinguish multiplicative from non-multiplicative functions or periodic from random coefficient structures in this context.
