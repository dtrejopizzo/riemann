## Overview The original hypothesis is REFUTED. The increased variance of Den between N=10⁵ and N=10⁶ is NOT driven by a more diverse distribution of dominant ω-class contributions (k_max). In fact, the Shannon entropy of the k_max distribution DECREASED from 1.5359 bits at N=10⁵ to 1.3114 bits at N=10⁶ (a 14.6% reduction), indicating LESS diversity in which class dominates at peaks. At N=10⁶, k=2 dominates 68% of peaks compared to only 44% at N=10⁵, showing increased concentration rather than diversification. However, the analysis reveals that the increased variance is driven by a fundamentally different mechanism: the diversification of power distribution WITHIN each peak across all ω-classes, not the diversity of which class dominates. Five independent quantitative metrics confirm this: 1. **Within-peak power distribution entropy increased significantly**: Mean H(P_k) increased from 1.9692 bits to 2.2852 bits (+16.0%, p<0.001), indicating that power is distributed more evenly across multiple ω-classes at each peak rather than being concentrated in one dominant class. 2. **Effective number of contributing classes increased**: Mean N_eff increased from 3.4123 to 4.2424 (+24.3%), meaning approximately one additional ω-class contributes significantly to the total power at N=10⁶ peaks. 3. **Variance contributions shifted to higher ω-classes**: The fraction of total variance contributed by k∈[4,5,6] nearly doubled from 25.5% to 46.4% (Δ=+20.9 percentage points), while k∈[1,2,3] dropped from 73.3% to 50.8% (Δ=-22.5 pp). This redistribution to higher, more variable classes directly increases Var(Den). 4. **Variance distribution entropy increased**: The Shannon entropy of how variance is distributed across the 10 ω-classes increased from 2.1583 to 2.2359 bits (+3.6%), confirming more classes contribute meaningfully to the total variance. 5. **Covariance structure shifted to higher k-pairs**: The dominant covariance term shifted from Cov(P₂,P₃) (13.3% contribution at N=10⁵) to Cov(P₃,P₄) (12.4% at N=10⁶), with covariances among k=3,4,5,6 becoming more prominent. Covariance terms continue to dominate (~78% of total variance at both scales). The mechanism is clear: at N=10⁶, peaks exhibit a more balanced power distribution across multiple ω-classes (higher N_eff, higher mean H(P_k)), with higher-k classes (k=4,5,6) gaining prominence. Since these higher classes have inherently larger variance in their P_k values across different peaks (coefficient of variation increases with k), their increased contribution directly amplifies Var(Den). The variance increase is not about WHICH class dominates varying across peaks, but about ALL classes contributing more equally, with higher-variance classes gaining weight. **DISCRETIONARY DECISIONS:**
