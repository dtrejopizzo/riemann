The calibration sharpens the picture from report r35: while r35 claimed ζ_δ_strong "is detectable" by Front I and Front III, this formal Step 0.5 calibration shows that only Front III genuinely produces a usable signal. Front I with 1/|ρ|² weights remains essentially blind because the weight w = 1/(re²+γ²) is dominated by γ² at all heights of interest, so even a 3× change in re produces only O(γ⁻⁴) ≈ 10⁻⁶ local weight perturbations that contribute < 10⁻⁹ to Jacobi coefficients — too close to the float64 noise floor (10⁻¹⁵) to constitute a useful detector after any realistic noise budget is applied. This is consistent with the structural blindness noted in the dataset description. Front III, by contrast, is a clean, linear, and predictable probe: the H0 bottleneck distance equals δ/2 exactly, because the time-delay embedding maps each "spike" in Re(ρ) to a cluster of points displaced by δ from the bulk at 0.5, and the H0 birth-death scale of those clusters is precisely δ. This gives a sharp, machine-precision-limited minimum detectable signal level. H1 should be treated separately — it captures perturbation geometry (contiguous spike → small loop) rather than magnitude, so it is unsuitable for δ-calibration but valuable for characterizing the spatial structure of any anomalies discovered. For the broader project, Step 0.5 thus authorizes Front III (real-part-aware TDA) as the calibrated detector for off-line excursions of any L-function ensemble, and recommends de-prioritizing 1/|ρ|²-weighted Front I as a real-part-sensitive observable. If Front I is to be retained as a real-part probe, an alternative weighting that does not decay as 1/γ² (e.g., weights of order O(1) or weights including a re-dependent prefactor that does not vanish at large γ) would be needed.
