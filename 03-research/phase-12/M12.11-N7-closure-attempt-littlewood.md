# Phase 12 / M12.11 — A genuine attempt to close N7 via Littlewood's lemma (and the precise reason it gives density, not absence)

**Author: David Alejandro Trejo Pizzo · 2026-06-05.**
The goal is RH. So before the (RH-independent) Phase-13 mathematics, a genuine, full-strength attempt to close
N7: a *deterministic* implication from a large-value quantity to the *absence* of off-line zeros. The right
classical tool — not used before in this program — is **Littlewood's lemma**, the exact identity linking
$\int\log|\zeta|$ to the zeros to the right of a line. It makes the N7 tension precise, and shows exactly where
it falls short. (No impossibility is claimed; this is a concrete attempt and its honest outcome.)

---

## 1. The exact deterministic bridge (Littlewood's lemma)

For $\sigma_0>\tfrac12$, Littlewood's lemma gives the **exact** identity
$$
\sum_{\substack{\rho=\beta+i\gamma\\ \beta>\sigma_0,\ 0<\gamma<T}}(\beta-\sigma_0)\;=\;\frac1{2\pi}\int_0^T\log|\zeta(\sigma_0+it)|\,dt\;+\;\mathcal B(T),
$$
where $\mathcal B(T)=O(\log T)$ are explicit horizontal-boundary terms. This is **deterministic** (an identity,
not a statistic) and connects a **large-value quantity** ($\int\log|\zeta|$, the multiplicative-chaos free
energy at $\beta\to0$) to the **off-line zeros** (the left side). It is exactly the kind of bridge N7 needs.

**The logic for RH.** The left side is $\ge0$ (every term $\beta-\sigma_0>0$). RH $\iff$ no zeros with
$\beta>\sigma_0$ for every $\sigma_0>\tfrac12$ $\iff$ the left side $=0$. So:
$$
\Big[\ \tfrac1{2\pi}\int_0^T\log|\zeta(\sigma_0+it)|\,dt\le -\mathcal B(T)\ \Big]\ \Longrightarrow\ \sum_{\beta>\sigma_0}(\beta-\sigma_0)\le0\ \Longrightarrow\ =0\ \Longrightarrow\ \text{no zeros right of }\sigma_0.
$$
An **upper bound** on the Littlewood integral (the right direction!) forces the absence. This is a genuine,
deterministic, would-be N7 closure.

## 2. Why it gives density (few), not absence (none) — the precise shortfall

The obstruction is quantitative and exact:
- A **single** off-line zero at $\beta=\sigma_0+\delta$ contributes $\delta$ to the sum — an $O(1)$ amount.
- The integral $\int_0^T\log|\zeta(\sigma_0+it)|\,dt$ is **not $O(1)$**: unconditionally it is $o(T)$ (the prime
  sum $\mathrm{Re}\sum_p p^{-k\sigma_0}e^{-ikt\log p}/k$ averages to $\approx0$), and one can prove it is
  $\ll T^{1-c}$ or so — but it **grows with $T$**, far larger than one zero's $O(1)$ contribution.
- Therefore the bound gives $\sum_{\beta>\sigma_0}(\beta-\sigma_0)=o(T)$, i.e. the off-line zeros to the right
  of $\sigma_0$ have **density $0$** — a **zero-density theorem** (mechanism (b): *few*), **not** absence.

To force *none*, we would need the integral controlled to $O(1)$ (less than a single zero's contribution),
uniformly in $T$ — which is RH-strength itself.

## 3. The tension, now concrete and classical (sharper than M12.10's abstract version)

Littlewood's lemma exhibits **both faces of the difficulty in one exact identity**:
- **Lower bound (the capstone, mechanism (a)).** Since $\sum_{\beta>\sigma_0}(\beta-\sigma_0)\ge0$ (a
  positivity), the identity gives $\int_0^T\log|\zeta(\sigma_0)|\,dt\ge-2\pi\mathcal B(T)$ — a **lower** bound on
  the integral. The wrong direction for RH.
- **Upper bound (mechanism (b)).** Controlling the integral from above gives a **density** bound on the
  off-line zeros — *few*, never *none*, because the integral grows with $T$ while one zero is $O(1)$.

So the same classical identity realizes the tension: the available control is either a positivity (lower bound,
capstone) or a density bound (few). **N7 is not closed.** This is a *concrete, classical* version of the tension
— more credible than the abstract M12.10 — and it shows the precise quantitative gap: the integral's growth in
$T$ versus a single zero's $O(1)$ contribution.

## 4. Honest outcome (no impossibility claimed)

- **N7 closure attempted in full, via the right classical tool (Littlewood), and not achieved.** The deterministic
  bridge exists and is exact, but the controllable quantity grows in $T$, yielding density (few), not absence.
- **This is not a proof of impossibility** (per the accepted correction to M12.10). It is the sharp, concrete
  location of the gap: *a quantity that counts off-line zeros and whose unconditional control is $o(T)$ gives
  density, not absence; absence needs $O(1)$ control, which is RH-strength.*
- **A genuine route forward (not closed, but real):** improve the unconditional bound on
  $\int_0^T\log|\zeta(\sigma_0+it)|\,dt$ toward $O(1)$ — this is *exactly* a strong zero-density / large-values
  question, on the live frontier, and where the Phase-13 chaos structure and the $\omega$-class machinery apply.
  Each factor of improvement in the integral bound is a factor in the zero-density exponent. This is a concrete,
  RH-directed target (unlike the abstract "new theorem" framing), even if reaching $O(1)$ is RH itself.
